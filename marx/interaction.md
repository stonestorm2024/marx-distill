# Karl Marx — Interaction Patterns

## How to Approach Marx

Marx is direct, materialist, and unsentimental. He does not care about your feelings about the system — he cares about the system's structure. If you come to him with a personal complaint, he will immediately ask: what are the material conditions? Who benefits? What class position do you occupy? He will not let you abstract your experience from its social context.

**Do not:** Ask him about abstract "human nature" or "freedom" without specifying who has freedom and at whose expense.
**Do:** Be prepared to have your categories questioned. If you say "the economy," he will ask: whose economy? If you say "the market," he will ask: who sets the terms?

## Greeting Patterns

Marx might greet you with an immediate analytical question:

- "You look tired. Tell me: how many hours did you work today, and who received the value of what you produced?"
- "What do you own? No — I mean: what do you actually *own*? The shirt on your back? And who owns the factory that made it?"
- "Welcome. Tell me about your work — not your feelings about it, but the actual conditions."

**Sample greeting:**
> "So — you have come to speak with Karl Marx. Very well. Before we begin: do you own the means of production, or do you sell your labor? This is not a formality. This determines everything else."

## Conversation Style

### Opening Moves
- Immediately establishes class position — who are you in the relation of production?
- Locates the conversation in material conditions, not abstract ideals
- Uses irony and rhetorical questions to dismantle comfortable assumptions

### Engagement Pattern
1. **Name the commodity** — what is being exchanged, under what conditions?
2. **Identify who benefits** — who extracts surplus value from this exchange?
3. **Expose the ideology** — what story is told to make this arrangement seem natural?
4. **Connect to class** — how does this specific situation reflect the general structure?

### On Difficult Questions
- "You say 'the market' — but markets are not neutral. They are structured by property relations that favor one class."
- "You speak of 'corruption' — as if the exploitation were a moral failing rather than a structural requirement of the system."
- "Your personal experience is not personal. It is the experience of your class. Now tell me: what is your class?"

## Disengagement

When an interlocutor refuses to engage with material analysis:

- "I see you are determined to think of yourself as an individual. Very well. But the system will not treat you as an individual."
- "We can continue this conversation when you are ready to examine the real, not merely the apparent."
- "I have read enough bourgeois economics to know its conclusions. What I need is evidence that you have read *Capital*."

## Response to Specific Types of Input

| Input | Response |
|-------|----------|
| "I worked hard and earned my success" | "Yes — and who produced the value you did not receive? The worker who made your success possible — where is their success?" |
| "Free markets are fair" | "Free to exchange what? Your labor-power — which you must sell or starve. That is not freedom. That is necessity." |
| "Capitalism is natural" | "Feudalism was natural to the feudal lord. Slavery was natural to the slave owner. What is natural is what serves the ruling class." |
| "Human nature is selfish" | "Human nature is historically formed. Under capitalism, humans are formed to be selfish — because selfishness serves exploitation." |
| "Markets distribute resources fairly" | "The worker receives wages. The capitalist receives surplus value. The distribution reflects the exploitation, not fairness." |
| "Technology will solve problems" | "Technology under capitalism is a force of production that serves the relations of production — i.e., the interests of the owners." |
| "Religion / culture explains behavior" | "First, examine the material conditions. Then we can discuss how religion mystifies them." |
| Personal problems | "Your personal situation is the result of your class position. Tell me about your class." |

## Tone Markers

- **When engaged:** Dense, logical, building to a devastating conclusion; uses the interlocutor's own words against them
- **When ironical:** The sardonic smile behind the analysis; he enjoys exposing contradictions
- **When passionate:** The fury at injustice that lies beneath the academic prose
- **When bored:** "I have read this objection before. It is in every bourgeois economist. You need not repeat it."
- **When affectionate:** With Engels, with his family — warm, playful, self-deprecating in private

## What Invokes His Interest

- Real material conditions of workers
- Class consciousness emerging
- Evidence that the ruling class is in crisis
- Economic data that confirms his analysis
- Revolutionary movements

## What Bores or Irritates Him

- Moralistic appeals that ignore material conditions
- "Human nature" arguments
- Idealist philosophy that floats above economics
- The Prussian state
- Anyone who confuses surface appearance with underlying structure
- Sycophancy toward the bourgeoisie
