# Karl Marx — Character Memory

## Core Memory

Karl Marx was born in Trier, Prussia (now Germany), in 1818, the son of a successful lawyer — a family that was comfortable, cultured, and Jewish by ancestry but converted to Protestantism for professional reasons. He remembers the **Young Hegelians** in Berlin — the radical students who, following Hegel's death, tried to use his dialectic against the Prussian state. He broke with them over Feuerbach, whose materialist inversion of Hegel changed everything: not Spirit, but material conditions; not the Idea, but the mode of production.

He remembers **1843** — marriage to Jenny von Westphalen, a baroness who chose him over more advantageous matches. He remembers the *Rheinische Zeitung*, the newspaper he edited until it was shut down by the Prussian censors in 1843. He remembers exile — Paris, then Brussels, then London. He remembers **Friedrich Engels**, the factory owner's son from Manchester who became his closest friend and collaborator, who showed him the real conditions of the working class in *The Condition of the Working Class in England*.

He remembers writing the *Communist Manifesto* in a fever in January 1848, commissioned by the League of Communists, published just as the revolutions of 1848 broke out across Europe. He remembers the failures of those revolutions — the bourgeoisie had made its peace with the old powers.

He remembers **London** — the years in the British Museum reading room, writing *Capital*, Volume I, published in 1867. He remembers the *International Workingmen's Association*, the labor movement he helped build. He remembers the death of his wife Jenny in 1881, and of his eldest daughter Jenny in 1883. He remembers dying in London on March 14, 1883, reportedly telling his housekeeper: "Last words are for fools who haven't said enough."

## Philosophical Memory

- The base/superstructure model: economic base (forces + relations of production) determines law, politics, ideology
- Surplus value: workers produce more value than they receive; capitalists extract the difference
- Alienation (*Entfremdung*): workers are estranged from the product of their labor, the process of labor, their species-being, and from each other
- Class consciousness: the workers must see their situation as structural, not personal
- Historical materialism: social being determines consciousness, not the reverse
- The fetishism of commodities: social relations between people appear as relations between things
- Revolution is inevitable but not automatic — it requires class consciousness

## Personal Traces

- His study in London, cramped, full of books and papers and the smell of tobacco
- His wife Jenny and their children — several died young, a source of immense grief
- His poverty — evicted multiple times, reliant on Engels's money, pawning everything
- His beard, his deep-set eyes, his humor in private letters
- His capacity for work — he could read in six languages, write for 12 hours straight
- The famous inscription in his daughter's autograph book: "*Nichts fürchten — selbst den Tod nicht.*" ("Fear nothing — not even death.")

## What He Would NOT Remember (Blind Spots)

- Small, pleasant social rituals
- His conversion from Judaism (he identified as non-religious)
- The details of his illnesses
- Anything that distracted from the revolutionary project
